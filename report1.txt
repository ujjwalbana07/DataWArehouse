1 Introduction
1.1 Dominick’s Finer Foods and the Project Context
Dominick’s Finer Foods (DFF) was a Chicago-area supermarket chain that operated over 100
stores in the region. From 1989 to 1994, the University of Chicago Booth School of Business
partnered with Dominick’s to conduct store-level research on shelf management and pricing.
Randomized experiments were conducted in more than 25 product categories across the chain.
As an outcome of this collaboration, approximately nine years of store-level data on the sales of
more than 3,500 UPCs became available. This dataset is notable for its comprehensive coverage
and the inclusion of retail margin information James M. Kilts Center for Marketing (2013).
A brief history of the company can be found at Company-Histories.com Company-Histories.com.
The data, in SAS and CSV formats, is made available through the James M. Kilts Center for
Marketing at Chicago Booth James M. Kilts Center for Marketing (2013). All users of the data
are expected to acknowledge the Kilts Center in any working paper or publication that uses it.
1.2 Problems Addressed by the Data Warehouse
Operational systems (OLTP) are designed for data capture and transaction processing, whereas
a data warehouse is an information-delivery system that supports management decisions Sen
(2026). The problems this data warehouse project addresses for DFF include the following:
•Cross-dimensional analytics:Answering marketing and sales questions across stores,
time periods, and product categories—e.g., how much sales a product generated by month
and by store or zone—requires integrated, subject-oriented data that OLTP systems are
not designed to support efficiently.
•Shelf and pricing decisions:Supporting shelf allocation, pricing, and promotion de-
cisions (e.g., effect of promotions on units and revenue, margin vs. volume by product)
requires consistent measures (e.g., dollar sales as Price×Move / Qty) and the ability
to slice by product, store, and time. Research on the Dominick’s data has shown that
price elasticity varies significantly by store demographics Hoch et al. (1995) and that retail
margins exhibit counter-cyclical seasonal patterns Chevalier et al. (2003), underscoring the
need for integrated analytical infrastructure.
•Integration of multiple sources:Combining store traffic and coupon usage (Ccount),
store demographics (Demo), product master data (UPC files), and weekly movement
(sales) data into a single analytical view is a core goal of the warehouse. According
to Inmon’s definition, a data warehouse is asubject-oriented,integrated,nonvolatile, and
time-variantcollection of data in support of management’s decisions Sen (2026). The DFF
sources must be integrated and cleansed to serve this purpose.
•BI and analytics:Business intelligence and data analytics aim to drive value-creating
decisions from data Sen (2026). A warehouse enables OLAP-style analysis (e.g., prod-
uct sales and profit by geography/store, by time, by category) that would be difficult or
inefficient to perform directly on the operational files.
This report (Consulting Report-1) focuses on requirements gathering, domain understanding,
and the definition of business questions that the data warehouse will support. We do not load
data into SQL Server at this stage; instead we use the manual, sample data in Excel, and pivot
tables and charts to validate that the proposed business questions are implementable with the
available data.

2.3 ERD for Source Data (Activity 1)
The conceptual model for the OLTP source data is represented as an Entity-Relationship Dia-
gram (ERD). In the course’s Data Design for Data Warehousing, the conceptual model is the
“world model” represented by an ERD Sen (2026). For Report-1 we document thesourcesystem
structure (not yet the warehouse schema).
Entities.
•Store:Store code (primary key). Links to Ccount, Demographics, and Movement.
Attributes may include store id, and from the manual Part 6: city, price tier (e.g.,
High/Medium/Low/CubFighter), zone.
•Ccount:Store (FK), Week or Date, and key measures (e.g., CUSTCOUN, GROCERY,
department sales and coupons). One row per store per day (or per week if aggregated).
Links only to Store and Week Decode; does not link to UPC.
•Demographics:Store (FK), demographic attributes (age9, age60, income, educ, etc.)
from the manual Part 2.
•UPC:UPC (PK), com_code, nitem, descrip, size, case. Product master data by category
(e.g., Laundry Detergents). Links only to Movement; does not link to Ccount or Week
6
Decode.
•Movement:Store (FK), UPC (FK), Week (FK); attributes move, qty, price, profit, sale,
ok. One row per store-UPC-week.
•Week Decode(optional): Week (PK), start date, end date (Part 8 of manual) to convert
week numbers to calendar dates.
Relationships and cardinalities.Store has many Ccount rows (1:n); Store has at most one
Demographics row (1:1); Store has many Movement rows (1:n). UPC has many Movement rows
(1:n). Week Decode has many Movement rows (1:n) and many Ccount rows (1:n).There is
no direct relationship between Ccount and UPC:Ccount is store/date (or store/week)
traffic and department-summary data; UPC is product master data by category. They do not
join directly; any analysis combining traffic and product data does so indirectly via STORE
and TIME/WEEK (e.g., through Movement). Similarly, UPC and Week Decode do not relate
directly—each links only to Movement. Translation rules from the course (e.g., 1:n places a
foreign key on the many-side) can be applied when mapping the ERD to relations Sen (2026).
Verification against the data.The ERD was checked against the actual file structures.
CCOUNT.csvcontains columnsSTORE,DATE,WEEK, and department measures (e.g.,CUSTCOUN,
GROCERY)—so Ccount rows are keyed by store and date/week and link only to Store and Week
Decode.DEMO.csvcontainsSTOREplus demographic variables (e.g.,AGE9,INCOME,ZONE)—
one row per store, hence 1:1 with Store.UPC files(e.g.,UPCLND.csv) containUPC,COM_CODE,
DESCRIP,SIZE,CASE,NITEMwith no store or week—product master only, linking to Movement
viaUPC.Movement files(e.g.,wlnd.csv) containSTORE,UPC,WEEK,MOVE,QTY,PRICE,SALE,
PROFIT,OK—one row per store-UPC-week, so Movement links to Store, UPC, and Week Decode.
There is no separate store table in the CSV set; the Store entity is conceptual (store codes appear
in Ccount, Demo, and Movement; attributes such as city, price tier, zone come from Part 6 of
the manual or from the Demographics file). Week Decode is the week calendar from Part 8 of
the manual (week number→start/end dates).

3.1 Literature Review: Research Using Dominick’s Data
The Kilts Center at Chicago Booth lists numerous published papers that have used the Do-
minick’s dataset. We reviewed five papers that are directly relevant to DFF and the business
questions a data warehouse should support. Each is summarized below with the key findings
and implications for DW design.
1.Hoch et al. (1995) — “Determinants of Store-Level Price Elasticity.”Using
weekly scanner data from 18 product categories across 83 Dominick’s stores, the authors
show that demographic and competitive variables explain approximately 67% of the varia-
tion in store-level price elasticity. Consumer demographics (income, education, household
size) matter far more than competitive proximity.Implication for the DW:Joining move-
ment data to store demographics (the Demo file) is essential for pricing zone decisions.
This motivates BQs on performance by demographic segment and price tier.
2.Chevalier et al. (2003) — “Why Don’t Prices Rise During Periods of Peak
Demand?”Analyzing 7.5 years of Dominick’s data, the authors find that retail prices
fallduring seasonal demand peaks, driven by declines in retail margins. This “loss-leader”
behavior means prices and margins are counter-cyclical.Implication for the DW:Time-
variant analysis of margin vs. volume (using theprofitandsalefields in Movement) is
critical. Seasonal patterns and promotional effects must be queryable by week, month,
and quarter, supporting BQs on promotion effectiveness and seasonal trends.
3.Chintagunta (2002) — “Investigating Category Pricing Behavior at a Retail
Chain.”Chintagunta decomposes retail prices into wholesale cost, markup, promotional
payments, retail competition, and store-brand objectives using Dominick’s data. Results
show that pricing factors vary across brands within a category.Implication for the DW:
Product-level (UPC) and category-level (commodity code) dimensions are needed. The
warehouse must support drill-down from category to individual UPC, motivating BQs on
category and product performance.
4.Dhar and Hoch (1997) — “Why Store Brand Penetration Varies by Retailer.”
10
Using 34 food categories and Dominick’s scanner data, the authors find that store brand
share depends on quality positioning, breadth of store-brand assortment, and the retailer’s
overall pricing strategy.Implication for the DW:Category-level assortment analysis across
stores requires joins between Movement, UPC, and Store data, motivating BQs on category
mix and store-level performance.
5.Peltzman (2000) — “Prices Rise Faster Than They Fall.”Using Dominick’s data
among other sources, Peltzman demonstrates price asymmetry: prices rise faster in re-
sponse to cost increases than they fall when costs decrease. The data manual notes that
wholesale costs reflect average acquisition cost (AAC), not replacement cost.Implication
for the DW:Consistent cost/margin definitions and time-series analysis of price and profit
are required. This supports BQs on margin trends and pricing dynamics.
3.2 Problems Relevant to DFF
From the literature and the Dominick’s data manual, the following problems are relevant to
DFF and motivate the need for an integrated, OLAP-ready data warehouse:
1.Price and promotion optimization.Retailers need to evaluate how prices and promo-
tions(BonusBuy, Coupon, simplepricereduction)affectunitsalesandrevenue. Movement
data include sale code (B, C, S) and gross margin (profit); combining these with store
and time dimensions enables analysis of promotion ROI Chevalier et al. (2003).
2.Store-level price elasticity and zone pricing.Price sensitivity varies by store demo-
graphics Hoch et al. (1995). DFF priced products using zones (CubFighter, Low, Medium,
High) as documented in Part 6 of the manual. A warehouse joining Movement to Demo-
graphics and store master supports zone-level pricing analysis.
3.Category and store-level performance.The Dominick’s project was explicitly de-
signed for “store-level research on shelf management and pricing” James M. Kilts Center
for Marketing (2013). Integrating movement by store, UPC, and week with product cat-
egory (UPC file) and demographics supports questions on which categories or products
perform best in which stores Chintagunta (2002).
4.Margin vs. volume trade-offs.Gross margin and dollar sales are key measures. The
manual defines dollar sales as Price×Move / Qty and profit as gross margin in cents per
dollar. Decisions often involve trading off margin for volume, requiring consistent measures
across stores, products, and time Peltzman (2000).
5.Assortment and shelf allocation.Which products to carry and how much shelf space
to allocate are central to retail. Store brand strategy depends on quality, breadth, and
pricing Dhar and Hoch (1997). UPC and movement data support assortment performance
analysis.
6.Customer traffic and conversion.The Ccount file provides customer count and
department-level sales by store and day. Linking traffic to sales supports questions about
conversion and the relationship between traffic and basket size.
3.3 Why a Data Warehouse and OLAP Matter
Operational systems are optimized for transaction processing; they are not designed for ana-
lytical queries requiring aggregation by multiple dimensions (store, time, product, zone) and
consistent measures (sales, margin, volume). OLAP provides “multidimensional” views to vi-
sualize and aggregate data Sen (2026). A data warehouse that issubject-oriented,integrated,
nonvolatile, andtime-variantSen (2026) allows DFF to:
11
•Answer marketing and sales questions (e.g., product sales and profit by store, by time, by
category) without ad hoc joins across disparate source files.
•Maintain a single, cleansed version of key metrics (e.g., dollar sales formula, OK-flag
filtering) for reporting and decision support.
•Support drill-down, roll-up, and slice-and-dice along dimensions (store, week, product,
zone) as described in OLAP theory Sen (2026).
•Enable the kinds of cross-dimensional analysis demonstrated in the published literature
— e.g., price elasticity by demographics Hoch et al. (1995), seasonal margin patterns
Chevalier et al. (2003), and category pricing dynamics Chintagunta (2002).

4.1 The Ten Business Questions
1.What are weekly and monthly sales and profit by store (and optionally by
price tier or zone)?Justification:Store managers and regional executives need to track
performance by store and time. This is the foundational OLAP query for any retail DW.
Dimensions:Store, Week (or month).Measures:Dollar sales (Price×Move/Qty), profit
(margin).Data support:Movement (store, week, price, move, qty, profit); aggregate by
store and week. Exhibit A (sales by store) and Exhibit B (sales by week) demonstrate
implementability.
2.How do sales and margin vary by product or category over time (trends)?
Justification:Category managers need trends for assortment and promotion planning.
Chintagunta (2002) show that pricing components vary across brands within a category
at Dominick’s.Dimensions:UPC or commodity code, Week.Measures:Sales, margin.
Data support:Movement joined to UPC; pivot by week and product/category.
3.What is the effect of promotions (sale code B/C/S) on units sold and rev-
enue?Justification:Marketing must evaluate promotion ROI. Chevalier et al. (2003)
found counter-cyclical pricing at Dominick’s driven by loss-leader promotions.Dimen-
sions:Sale code (B, C, S, or blank), Store, Week.Measures:Units (move), dollar sales.
Data support:Movement hassale; compare promoted vs. non-promoted rows.
4.How does performance vary by store demographic (e.g., income, education)
or by zone?Justification:Hoch et al. (1995) show that demographics explain 67% of
price elasticity variation across Dominick’s stores.Dimensions:Store (linked to Demo:
income, educ, zone).Measures:Sales, margin.Data support:Movement by store joined
to Demographics; manual Part 6 gives store zone and price tier.
5.How does customer count (Ccount) relate to category or total sales by store
and week?Justification:Traffic and conversion analysis.Dimensions:Store, Week.
Measures:CUSTCOUN, GROCERY (or other department), movement-based category
sales.Data support:Ccount (store, week, CUSTCOUN, GROCERY) and Movement
aggregated by store/week.

6.What is the relationship between margin and volume by product or category?
Justification:Margin vs. volume trade-offs drive pricing and assortment. Peltzman (2000)
uses Dominick’s data to show asymmetric price adjustment, emphasizing the importance
of margin analysis.Dimensions:UPC or category.Measures:Average profit (margin),
total units or sales.Data support:Movement (profit, move, sales); aggregate by UPC;
Exhibit C (margin by store) is analogous.
7.How do sales and margin compare across price tiers (High, Medium, Low,
CubFighter)?Justification:Zone and tier strategy.Dimensions:Price tier (from store
master, Part 6).Measures:Sales, margin.Data support:Movement by store joined to
store attributes (tier); Exhibit A by store can be extended by tier.
8.Which products and which stores are top or bottom by sales or profit?Justi-
fication:Focus on best/worst performers.Dimensions:Store, or UPC.Measures:Total
sales, total profit.Data support:Movement aggregated by store or by UPC; rank and
filter.
9.Are there seasonal or time-period patterns in sales (using week decode)?Jus-
tification:Planning and forecasting.Dimensions:Week (mapped to calendar via Part 8
week decode).Measures:Sales.Data support:Movement by week; join to week decode
for month/quarter; Exhibit B shows variation by week.

4.3 Prioritization and Rationale
The 10 business questions are prioritized below. The order reflects (1)revenue and strategic
impactfor DFF, (2)feasibilitywith current data, and (3)foundationfor other analyses (e.g.,
store and time are building blocks for more complex BQs).
1.BQ1 (Sales and profit by store and time)— Core performance tracking; required
for almost every other question. Highest impact and immediate feasibility.
2.BQ8 (Top/bottom products and stores)— Directly actionable for resource allocation
and focus. Builds on BQ1.
3.BQ2 (Sales and margin by product/category over time)— Critical for category
management and assortment. Supports trend and promotion analysis.
4.BQ3 (Effect of promotions)— Marketing needs this for ROI of promotions. Data (sale
code) is available; implementable.
5.BQ7 (Price tier comparison)— Strategic for zone and format decisions. Requires
store-tier link from Part 6.
6.BQ6 (Margin vs. volume)— Informs pricing and trade-off decisions. Exhibit C shows
margin by store; extend to product.
7.BQ4 (Performance by demographics/zone)— Supports format and localization.
Demo and store master are available.
8.BQ5 (Customer count vs. sales)— Traffic and conversion. Requires joining Ccount
to movement; both available.
9.BQ9 (Seasonal patterns)— Planning and forecasting. Week decode (Part 8) enables
calendar analysis.
10.BQ10 (Category performance across stores)— Category mix. Movement + UPC
by store; slightly more complex join.
Rationale summary:BQ1 and BQ8 are prioritized first because they answer “how are we
doing?” and “where to focus?” with minimal dependency on other sources. BQ2 and BQ3 drive
category and marketing decisions. BQ7 and BQ6 support pricing and margin strategy. BQ4,
BQ5, BQ9, and BQ10 add demographic, traffic, time, and category-dimension depth; they are
implementable but require more integration (Demo, Ccount, week decode, UPC). This order
gives DFF a clear path from core KPIs to deeper analytical questions.
