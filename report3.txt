1. Introduction 
1.1 Dominick's Finer Foods and Project Context 
Dominick's Finer Foods (DFF) was a Chicago-area supermarket chain operating over 100 stores. From 1989 
to 1994, the University of Chicago Booth School of Business partnered with Dominick's to conduct store-
level research on shelf management and pricing across more than 25 product categories. Approximately 
eight years of store-level data covering over 3,500 UPCs became available through the James M. Kilts 
Center for Marketing (2013). 
1.2 Problems Addressed by the Data Warehouse 
Operational OLTP systems are optimized for transaction processing, not analytical queries (Sen, 2026). This 
data warehouse addresses four core problems for DFF: (1) cross-dimensional analytics across stores, time, 
and products, (2) shelf and pricing decisions requiring consistent measures like dollar sales = Price × Move / 
Qty, (3) integration of multiple disparate source files (Movement, Demographics, UPC, Customer Count) 
into a single analytical view, and (4) enabling OLAP-style business intelligence (drill-down, roll-up, slice-
and-dice) that is impractical on normalized OLTP schemas. 
1.3 Understanding of the Data 
Four primary CSV source files are used: 
• wlnd.csv (Movement): Weekly store-UPC transactions for Laundry Detergents. Key fields: STORE, 
UPC, WEEK, MOVE, QTY, PRICE, SALE, PROFIT, OK. 
• upclnd.csv (UPC): Product master data. Key fields: UPC, COM_CODE, DESCRIP, SIZE, CASE, 
NITEM. 
• demo.csv (Demographics): One row per store with census-based variables. Key fields: STORE, 
ZONE, INCOME (log of median income), EDUC. 
• ccount.csv (Customer Count): Store-level customer traffic and department sales. Key fields: 
STORE, WEEK, CUSTCOUN, GROCERY, and department-level columns.

2. Subject Area Understanding 
2.1 Literature Review 
Five papers using Dominick's data were reviewed: 
Hoch et al. (1995) found demographics explain ~67% of store-level price elasticity. DW implication: Join 
Movement to Demographics for zone pricing. 
Chevalier et al. (2003) found counter-cyclical pricing — retail margins fall during demand peaks. DW 
implication: Time-variant margin/volume analysis required. 
Chintagunta (2002) decomposed retail prices showing pricing factors vary across brands. DW implication: 
Product and category dimensions needed. 
Dhar & Hoch (1997) found store brand share depends on quality, breadth, and pricing. DW implication: 
Category assortment analysis across stores. 
Peltzman (2000) demonstrated asymmetric price adjustment using Dominick's data. DW implication: 
Consistent cost/margin definitions and time-series capability. 
2.2 Problems Relevant to DFF 
Six problems motivate the warehouse: (1) price/promotion optimization, (2) store-level price elasticity and 
zone pricing, (3) category and store performance tracking, (4) margin vs. volume trade-offs, (5) assortment 
and shelf allocation, (6) customer traffic and conversion analysis.

3. Overview of Kimball's Methodology 
The design follows Ralph Kimball's Bottom-Up Dimensional Modeling methodology (Kimball & Ross, 
2013). The four-step process: 
Step 1 — Select the Business Process. Identify the real-world operational event generating measurable 
data. 
Step 2 — Declare the Grain. Define what one row in the fact table represents. This is the most critical step 
— incorrect grain causes double-counting. 
Step 3 — Identify the Dimensions. Determine the "who, what, where, when, how" context for filtering and 
grouping. 
Step 4 — Identify the Facts. Isolate numeric measures classified by additivity: additive (safe to SUM 
across all dimensions), semi-additive (across some), non-additive (ratios — use AVG only). 
Kimball's star schema minimizes joins for analytical queries. Conformed dimensions shared across data 
marts enable drill-across queries. This bus architecture suits DFF because the two business processes 
(checkout and traffic) operate at different grains and must be linked through shared Dim_Store and 
Dim_Date dimensions. 
 
4. Data Warehouse Logical Design 
4.1 Application of Kimball Methodology 
Process A — Weekly POS Checkout (wlnd.csv): Records units sold, price, margin, and promotion per 
product per store per week. Supports BQ1–BQ4. 
Process B — Facility Entrance Counting (ccount.csv): Records total customer traffic per store per week. 
Supports BQ5. 
Grain: 
• Fact_Weekly_Sales: One row per Store × Product (UPC) × Week 
• Fact_Customer_Traffic: One row per Store × Week 
Why two fact tables are mandatory: If traffic counts (Store × Week) were embedded into the sales table 
(Store × Product × Week), each store's weekly customer count would replicate across every product row. 
Example: Store #5 has 10,000 visitors and 5,000 products in Week 278. Merging → SUM(customer_count) 
= 50,000,000 instead of 10,000. This grain-violation error makes BQ5 unanswerable. 
4.2 Dimension Bus Matrix 
Business Process Dim_Store Dim_Date Dim_Product Dim_Promotion BQs 
Fact_Weekly_Sales ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ BQ1–BQ4 
Fact_Customer_Traffic ✓ ✓ — — BQ5 
Dim_Store and Dim_Date are conformed across both facts (enabling drill-across for BQ5). 
4.3 Grain Definition 
Fact_Weekly_Sales: One row = one Store × one UPC × one Week. Fact_Customer_Traffic: One row = 
one Store × one Week. 
4.4 Dimension and Fact Identification 
Dim_Store (Conformed): store_id, store_zone, median_income (EXP of log-income), education_pct, 
income_band (High/Medium/Low). Source: DEMO.csv. 
Dim_Date (Conformed): week_id, calendar_date, calendar_month, calendar_quarter, calendar_year, 
season, holiday_flag. Derived from epoch + (WEEK_ID−1) × 7 days, epoch = Sept 14, 1989. 
Dim_Product: upc_id, item_description, commodity_code (denormalized — no snowflaking). Source: 
UPCLND.csv. 
Dim_Promotion: sale_code (B/C/S/NONE), promotion_type. 4 rows. sale_code = 'NONE' is a valid 
business state (no promotion); SK = −1 is an ETL lookup failure (Unknown Member). 
Fact_Weekly_Sales measures: units_sold (Additive), retail_price (Non-additive), dollar_sales (Additive, 
derived), profit_margin_pct (Non-additive — stored as cents per dollar, use AVG). 
Fact_Customer_Traffic measures: customer_count (Additive).

4.6 Schema Justification for BQ1–BQ5 
BQ1: SUM(dollar_sales), AVG(profit_margin_pct) from Fact_Weekly_Sales, joined to Dim_Store and 
Dim_Date. Roll-up from Store×Product×Week to Store×Quarter. 
BQ2: SUM(units_sold), SUM(dollar_sales), AVG(profit_margin_pct) joined to Dim_Product 
(commodity_code) and Dim_Date. Trend analysis by category over time. 
BQ3: SUM(units_sold), SUM(dollar_sales) joined to Dim_Promotion. Dice by promotion_type to compare 
promoted vs. non-promoted. 
BQ4: SUM(dollar_sales), AVG(profit_margin_pct) joined to Dim_Store (income_band, store_zone). Dice 
by demographic segment. 
BQ5: Drill-across — pre-aggregate Fact_Weekly_Sales to Store×Week (eliminating product dimension via 
SUM), then join to Fact_Customer_Traffic on conformed Dim_Store and Dim_Date keys. No fan-out 
because both are at Store×Week after aggregation.

4.9 Physical Design Plan 
Both fact tables use Clustered Columnstore Indexes (CCI) for 10:1 compression and batch-mode 
aggregation. Nonclustered B-tree indexes are overlaid on store_sk and date_sk for ETL point-lookups. Fact 
tables are partitioned by calendar_year on date_sk. Materialized Indexed Views pre-aggregate monthly store 
revenue and weekly store sales for dashboard performance. Dimension tables use SCD Type 1 (overwrite), 
appropriate since demographics are static 1990 Census data. All ETL uses SSIS with incremental append via 
WHERE date_sk > MAX(existing). 
4.10 Star Schema ERD 
>>> INSERT: Visio star schema ERD diagram (Figure 6) <<< 
The ERD shows two central fact tables (Fact_Weekly_Sales and Fact_Customer_Traffic) surrounded by 
four dimensions. Dim_Store and Dim_Date are conformed (shared). Dim_Product and Dim_Promotion 
connect only to Fact_Weekly_Sales. All relationships are 1:M from dimension to fact.

5.5 Data Transformation and Cleansing Rules 
Rule T1: OK Flag Filtering 
• SSIS Component: Conditional Split (in Load stg_Movement Data Flow) 
• Condition for valid output: OK_FLAG == 1 && MOVE > 0 && PRICE > 0 
• Effect: Rows with corrupt data (OK = 0), zero/negative movement, or zero/negative price are 
excluded before reaching the staging table. 
Rule T2: SALE Code Standardization 
• SSIS Component: Derived Column (in Load stg_Movement Data Flow) 
• Expression: ISNULL(SALE) || TRIM(SALE) == "" ? "NONE" : UPPER(TRIM(SALE)) 
• Effect: Blank/null SALE values become "NONE" (valid business state = no promotion). All codes 
uppercased and whitespace-trimmed. Valid output values: B, C, S, NONE. 
Rule T3: QTY Null/Zero Handling 
• SSIS Component: Derived Column (in Load stg_Movement Data Flow) 
• Expression: ISNULL(QTY) || QTY <= 0 ? 1 : QTY 
• Rationale: QTY is the divisor in dollar_sales = (PRICE × MOVE) / QTY. Null/zero QTY would 
cause division-by-zero. Default 1 means single-unit pricing (no bundle). 
Rule T4: PROFIT Null Handling 
• SSIS Component: Derived Column 
• Expression: ISNULL(PROFIT) ? 0.00 : PROFIT 
• Effect: NULL margin values default to 0.00 cents per dollar. 
Rule T5: DESCRIP Special Character Cleansing 
• SSIS Component: Derived Column (in Load stg_UPC Data Flow) 
• Expression: 
TRIM(REPLACE(REPLACE(REPLACE(REPLACE(REPLACE(DESCRIP,"#",""),"~",""),"<",""),"
$",""),"*","")) 
• Rationale: DFF manual documents special characters: # = combo-store sale, ~ = discontinued, < = 
trial size, $ and * = no meaning. Removed for clean reporting names. 
• Null handling: ISNULL(result) || result == "" ? "Unknown" : result 
Rule T6: INCOME Log-to-Dollar Conversion 
• SSIS Component: OLE DB Source SQL query (in Load Dim_Store Data Flow) 
• SQL Expression: EXP(LOG_INCOME) AS MEDIAN_INCOME_DOLLARS 
• Rationale: DEMO.csv INCOME field stores ln(median household income). EXP() recovers the 
dollar value. Verification: INCOME = 10.9 → EXP(10.9) ≈ $54,176. 
• This is a special ETL technique — see Section 6.11. 
Rule T7: Income Band Derivation 
• SSIS Component: Derived Column (in Load Dim_Store Data Flow) 
• Expression: MEDIAN_INCOME_DOLLARS >= 60000 ? "High" : 
(MEDIAN_INCOME_DOLLARS >= 35000 ? "Medium" : "Low") 
• Effect: Converts raw dollar income into analyst-friendly categories for GROUP BY operations 
without requiring end-users to define income thresholds. 
Rule T8: Dollar Sales Computation 
• SSIS Component: Derived Column (in Load Fact_Weekly_Sales Data Flow) 
• Expression: (DT_DECIMAL,2)((PRICE * (DT_DECIMAL,2)MOVE) / (DT_DECIMAL,2)QTY) 
• Rationale: Standard DFF revenue formula. QTY = pricing bundle quantity (e.g., 2-for-$5 deal → 
QTY = 2, PRICE = $5, dollar_sales = $5 × units / 2). 
• This is a special ETL technique — see Section 6.11. 
Rule T9: Dim_Date Calendar Derivation 
• SSIS Component: Execute SQL Task 
• Logic: Collect DISTINCT week IDs from stg_Movement UNION stg_Ccount. For each: 
calendar_date = DATEADD(WEEK, WEEK_ID − 1, '1989-09-14'). Derive month, quarter, year, 
season (3–5=Spring, 6–8=Summer, 9–11=Fall, 12/1/2=Winter), and holiday_flag 
(Thanksgiving/Christmas weeks). 
Rule T10: Surrogate Key Lookup with Unknown Member Fallback 
• SSIS Component: Lookup Transformation (4 instances in Fact_Weekly_Sales, 2 in 
Fact_Customer_Traffic) 
• Configuration: "Redirect rows to no match output" — unmatched rows are assigned SK = −1 via 
Derived Column, then Union All merges matched and unmatched rows. 
• This is a special ETL technique — see Section 6.11. 
Rule T11: Customer Count Weekly Aggregation 
• SSIS Component: OLE DB Source with GROUP BY 
• SQL: SELECT STORE_ID, WEEK_ID, SUM(CUSTOMER_COUNT) AS CUSTOMER_COUNT 
FROM stg_Ccount GROUP BY STORE_ID, WEEK_ID 
• Rationale: CCOUNT.csv may contain daily rows. Fact_Customer_Traffic grain is Store × Week, so 
daily values must be aggregated to weekly. 
5.6 Plan for Aggregate Tables 
Three aggregate tables (implemented as Materialized Indexed Views in SQL Server) are planned to 
accelerate frequently-run dashboard queries: 
Aggregate 1: AGG_Monthly_Store_Revenue (supports BQ1 monthly dashboards) 
Pre-computes SUM(dollar_sales) by store_sk, calendar_year, calendar_month. Eliminates full fact table 
scan for monthly performance reports. 
CREATE VIEW dbo.AGG_Monthly_Store_Revenue WITH SCHEMABINDING AS 
SELECT  
    f.store_sk, 
    d.calendar_year, 
    d.calendar_month, 
    SUM(f.dollar_sales) AS total_revenue, 
    SUM(f.units_sold) AS total_units, 
    COUNT_BIG(*) AS row_count 
FROM dbo.Fact_Weekly_Sales f 
INNER JOIN dbo.Dim_Date d ON f.date_sk = d.date_sk 
GROUP BY f.store_sk, d.calendar_year, d.calendar_month; 
GO 
CREATE UNIQUE CLUSTERED INDEX IX_AGG_Monthly  
ON dbo.AGG_Monthly_Store_Revenue(store_sk, calendar_year, calendar_month); 
  
Aggregate 2: AGG_Weekly_Store_Sales (supports BQ5 drill-across) 
Pre-aggregates Fact_Weekly_Sales to Store × Week grain, eliminating product dimension. Critical for BQ5, 
which requires joining sales to traffic at matching grain. 
CREATE VIEW dbo.AGG_Weekly_Store_Sales WITH SCHEMABINDING AS 
SELECT  
    f.store_sk, 
    f.date_sk, 
    SUM(f.dollar_sales) AS total_revenue, 
    SUM(f.units_sold) AS total_units, 
    COUNT_BIG(*) AS row_count 
FROM dbo.Fact_Weekly_Sales f 
GROUP BY f.store_sk, f.date_sk; 
GO 
CREATE UNIQUE CLUSTERED INDEX IX_AGG_Weekly  
ON dbo.AGG_Weekly_Store_Sales(store_sk, date_sk); 
  
Aggregate 3: AGG_Promotion_Summary (supports BQ3 promotion analysis) 
CREATE VIEW dbo.AGG_Promo_Performance WITH SCHEMABINDING AS 
SELECT  
    f.promotion_sk, 
    SUM(f.dollar_sales) AS total_revenue,

5.7 Organization of Data Staging Area 
The staging database Ujjwal_Group3_Staging_Area is a transient workspace between source files and the

staging → Load into warehouse → Drop staging tables.

Staging Principles: 
1. All staging tables are TRUNCATED before each ETL run (idempotent loading). 
2. Source-level cleansing (OK filtering, null handling, type conversion) occurs during source → 
staging. 
3. Business-level transformations (EXP(INCOME), income_band, dollar_sales, surrogate key lookups) 
occur during staging → data mart. 
4. After all DW tables are loaded and validated, all staging tables are DROPPED.

INSERT INTO Dim_Date (week_id, calendar_date, calendar_month, calendar_quarter,  
                      calendar_year, season, holiday_flag) 
SELECT DISTINCT 
    w.WEEK_ID, 
    DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch), 
    MONTH(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)), 
    DATEPART(QUARTER, DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)), 
    YEAR(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)), 
    CASE  
        WHEN MONTH(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)) IN (3,4,5) THEN 'Spring'

        WHEN MONTH(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)) IN (9,10,11) THEN 'Fall' 
        ELSE 'Winter' 
    END, 
    CASE 
        WHEN MONTH(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)) = 11  
             AND DAY(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)) BETWEEN 22 AND 28 THEN 1 
        WHEN MONTH(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)) = 12  
             AND DAY(DATEADD(WEEK, w.WEEK_ID - 1, @epoch)) BETWEEN 20 AND 31 THEN 1 
        ELSE 0

6.6 Data Granularity at the Independent Data Mart Level 
Data granularity is the level of detail stored in each fact table. Correct granularity prevents double-counting 
and ensures aggregation accuracy. 
Fact_Weekly_Sales granularity: One row per Store × Product (UPC) × Week. This is the finest grain 
available from the Movement source file. Each row represents the complete sales record for one specific 
product at one specific store during one specific week. 
Fact_Customer_Traffic granularity: One row per Store × Week. This is the only grain available from 
CCOUNT data, which provides a single customer count per store per week with no product-level 
breakdown. 
Why grain isolation matters: The two grains are incompatible. Mixing Store × Product × Week data with 
Store × Week data in a single table would cause the Store × Week measure (customer_count) to replicate 
across every product row, producing inflated totals. Cross-fact analysis for BQ5 uses the Kimball drill-
across pattern: pre-aggregate Fact_Weekly_Sales to Store × Week (eliminating the product dimension), then 
join to Fact_Customer_Traffic on the conformed Dim_Store and Dim_Date keys. 
Granularity verification queries: 
-- Fact_Weekly_Sales: should return 0 rows (no duplicates at grain) 
SELECT store_sk, product_sk, date_sk, COUNT(*) AS cnt 
FROM Fact_Weekly_Sales 
GROUP BY store_sk, product_sk, date_sk 
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1; 
 
-- Fact_Customer_Traffic: should return 0 rows (no duplicates at grain) 
SELECT store_sk, date_sk, COUNT(*) AS cnt 
FROM Fact_Customer_Traffic 
GROUP BY store_sk, date_sk 
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

BQ5: Customer Count vs. Sales (Kimball Drill-Across) 
WITH SalesAgg AS ( 
    SELECT f.store_sk, f.date_sk, 
        SUM(f.dollar_sales) AS total_revenue, SUM(f.units_sold) AS total_units 
    FROM Fact_Weekly_Sales f 
    GROUP BY f.store_sk, f.date_sk 
), 
TrafficData AS ( 
    SELECT t.store_sk, t.date_sk, t.customer_count 
    FROM Fact_Customer_Traffic t 
) 
SELECT s.store_id, s.store_zone, s.income_band, 
    d.week_id, d.calendar_year, d.calendar_quarter, 
    sa.total_revenue, sa.total_units, td.customer_count, 
    CASE WHEN td.customer_count > 0  
         THEN CAST(sa.total_revenue / td.customer_count AS DECIMAL(10,2)) ELSE 0  
    END AS revenue_per_visitor, 
    CASE WHEN td.customer_count > 0  
         THEN CAST(CAST(sa.total_units AS FLOAT) / td.customer_count AS DECIMAL(10,4)) ELSE 0  
    END AS units_per_visitor 
FROM SalesAgg sa 
INNER JOIN TrafficData td ON sa.store_sk = td.store_sk AND sa.date_sk = td.date_sk 
INNER JOIN Dim_Store s ON sa.store_sk = s.store_sk 
INNER JOIN Dim_Date d ON sa.date_sk = d.date_sk 
WHERE s.store_sk != -1 AND d.date_sk != -1 
ORDER BY revenue_per_visitor DESC; 
  
Why BQ5 works correctly: The CTE SalesAgg pre-aggregates Fact_Weekly_Sales from Store × Product × 
Week to Store × Week (eliminating the product dimension). Only then does the join to 
Fact_Customer_Traffic occur. Both sides produce one row per Store × Week, so no fan-out or double-
counting occurs. This is the Kimball drill-across pattern enabled by conformed Dim_Store and Dim_Date.

6.11 Special ETL Techniques Implemented 
This section highlights notable techniques used in the ETL that go beyond standard copy-and-load 
operations. 
Special Technique 1: Log-Income to Dollar Conversion (EXP Transformation) 
The INCOME field in DEMO.csv stores the natural logarithm of median household income (e.g., INCOME 
= 10.9). To recover the actual dollar value for meaningful analysis, we apply EXP() during the Dim_Store 
loading Data Flow: 
SELECT EXP(LOG_INCOME) AS MEDIAN_INCOME_DOLLARS FROM stg_Demo; 
-- Example: EXP(10.9) = $54,176.35 
  
This transformation is performed in the OLE DB Source SQL query rather than in a Derived Column to 
leverage SQL Server's native EXP() function with full decimal precision. The resulting dollar value feeds 
into the income_band Derived Column (High ≥ $60K, Medium ≥ $35K, Low < $35K). 
Special Technique 2: Multi-Pack QTY Normalization in Dollar Sales 
The dollar_sales formula — (PRICE × MOVE) / QTY — normalizes multi-buy pricing. QTY represents the 
pricing bundle quantity: a 2-for-$5.00 deal has PRICE = $5.00 and QTY = 2. Without dividing by QTY, 
revenue would be overstated for bundled products. This Derived Column expression in the 
Fact_Weekly_Sales Data Flow ensures accurate revenue: 
SSIS Expression: (DT_DECIMAL,2)((PRICE * MOVE) / QTY) 
  
The QTY default of 1 (from Rule T3) prevents division-by-zero for null/zero values. 
Special Technique 3: Unknown Member Surrogate Key Pattern 
Following Kimball best practice, all four dimension tables are pre-loaded with an Unknown Member record 
at surrogate key −1 before any fact loading begins. During fact loading, the four Lookup Transformations 
are configured with "Ignore failure" for unmatched rows, producing NULL surrogate keys. A post-load 
Execute SQL Task then replaces all NULL SKs with −1: 
UPDATE Fact_Weekly_Sales SET store_sk = -1 WHERE store_sk IS NULL; 
-- (repeated for date_sk, product_sk, promotion_sk, and traffic fact) 
  
This ensures: (1) no transaction is silently dropped, (2) analysts can query WHERE store_sk = -1 to audit 
unresolved references, and (3) late-arriving dimension data can be reconciled without reprocessing historical 
loads. 
Special Technique 4: Drill-Across Implementation for BQ5 
BQ5 requires combining data from two fact tables at different grains. The implementation uses the Kimball 
drill-across pattern with CTEs: 
1. Pre-aggregate Fact_Weekly_Sales from Store × Product × Week to Store × Week (SUM of 
dollar_sales and units_sold), eliminating the product dimension. 
2. Join the aggregated sales to Fact_Customer_Traffic on conformed store_sk and date_sk. 
3. Compute revenue_per_visitor and units_per_visitor as derived conversion metrics. 
This ensures customer_count is never duplicated across product rows, maintaining mathematical accuracy. 
Special Technique 5: Conditional Split with Row Count Auditing 
In the stg_Movement Data Flow, the Conditional Split component routes rows into two outputs: ValidRows 
(OK = 1 AND MOVE > 0 AND PRICE > 0) and RejectedRows (everything else). The rejected output 
connects to a Row Count transformation that stores the count in SSIS variable @RejectedRowCount. This 
enables post-execution auditing: we can report exactly how many source rows were excluded for data 
quality reasons, providing transparency into the ETL pipeline's filtering behavior.

The DFF Data Warehouse transforms fragmented CSV files into an integrated analytical platform:

10. Appendix: Assumptions and Technical Notes 
A1. Revenue Formula: Dollar sales = (PRICE × MOVE) / QTY. QTY = pricing bundle quantity (2-for-$5 
→ QTY = 2). 
A2. Temporal Alignment: WEEK values in CCOUNT.csv use the same numbering system as Movement 
files. 
A3. Dim_Date Epoch: Week 1 = September 14, 1989. Calendar attributes derived via DATEADD(WEEK, 
WEEK_ID − 1, epoch). 
A4. SCD Type 1: Demographics are static 1990 Census values. Overwrite strategy used. 
A5. EXP(INCOME): INCOME in DEMO.csv = ln(median household income). EXP() recovers dollar 
values. 
A6. Profit Storage: profit_margin_pct stored as cents-per-dollar (25.30 = 25.30% margin). Use AVG(), not 
SUM(). 
A7. OK Filter: OK = 0 rows permanently excluded. MOVE ≤ 0 and PRICE ≤ 0 also rejected. 
A8. Unknown Members: SK = −1 reserved in all dimensions for unresolved lookups. 
A9. SSIS-Only: All ETL performed exclusively in SSIS. No Python, Java, or Excel used for 
transformations. 
A10. Database Names: Staging = Ujjwal_Group3_Staging_Area. Data Warehouse = 
Ujjwal_Group3_DW_Area. Both on SQL Server 2016.
